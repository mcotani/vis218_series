Kimono & Yukata

A classic, a timeless piece. Very easily recognizeable. Kimonos and yukatas (a cotton, lightweight version of the former) were the everyday clothes worn by both men and women. But with the westernization of Japan in the Meiji period, the kimonos came to be seen as more "feminine" (that is, the Europeans convinced the Japanese that they were), prompting a switch from men towards more western business suits. I appreciate how versatile and efficient this cut is. I did not know about the disassemble and resew part, which apparently today is much less common since it’s so laborious. However, sewing, especially with a sewing machine, the kimono together is pretty straightforward, especially today with a sewing machine, and I wonder what cool things we could do if we invested in really good, durable fabric, to create pieces that could follow us as we grow (thinking of children here) or be passed down to our own children in the future. While the proportions of some of the cuts have changed, the general silhouette has been the same for centuries. Neat.

Fabrication

The kimono is super efficient, it is cut out of a singe long, rectangular strip of fabric. You use the entire strip, almost no waste, with all rectangular cuts which make it super easy to assemble as well. Historically, the seam allowances are flexible and not trimmed down, allowing kimonos and yukatas to be easily disassembled and re-sewn at larger/smaller sizes, depending on what is necessary.
